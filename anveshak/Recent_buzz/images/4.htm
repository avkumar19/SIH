          AI may not lead to job losses, but will force people to upskill themselves

          Digbijay Mishra, Sujit John
          | TNN | Updated: Feb 25, 2019, 08:45 IST

          TimesPoints

          81
          0 Added

          Claim your

          1 Point

          Sebastian Thrun, a name as popular in Silicon Valley as it is in its Indian counterpart, Bengaluru, at 51 is in middle of running three startups. Germany-born Thrun has previously worked with Google and is known as the architect of the search-engine giant’s self-driving initiative and the secretive research lab-Google X. Around four months back, the AI scientist made a comeback to the day-to-day operations at his startup Udacity following a restructuring at the company when his startup’s CEO had to step down. Thrun feels he is seeing an indication of his comeback as interim CEO at Udacity starting to work. He spoke to TOI’s Digbijay Mishra and Sujit John over what’s next for his startups from e-learning to flying cars, AI’s compatibility with human evolution and the need for life-long learning. Edited Excerpts:


          How are you approaching the Indian market now?

          We want to double down on enterprise and consumer both, there are strong synergies. There are total 36,000 students in India accumulatively. Enterprise is still a small business which we started in the middle of last year. But it’s been growing, we have great collaborations with Infosys, Wipro, Flipkart and so on. It’s been growing 100% worldwide. More companies are deciding, instead of hiring from outside which is very hard, to ask us to develop talent internally.

          Going forward, how do you see this playing out in India, both onthe consumer as well as enterprise side?
          India has always been one of the most active countries. Bengaluru has been the most active city for Udacity since inception. (Maximum number of people accessing free courses here). This is more than San Francisco and New York. So, to me, it speaks of the enormous thirst for education in India.

          How many paying individual students do you have here and what proportion of students are taking AI course?

          Here, the number of students taking free courses is around 1.15 million but the number of paying students is increasing quite a bit.

          At this point, half of the people are taking AI courses. (There is also high interest from Indians to learn self-driving and autonomous cars in India.)

          With AI, there is always a concern about job loss. I read you saying AI, like other technologies, is a machine that strengthens the human being. Taking a macro view, how do you see AI impacting jobs globally?

          I think, for any individual, it’s not ok to rest on your laurels. I think we are entering an age of life-long learning which means as new technologies come, AI in particular, people will be forced to upskill themselves. However, globally we are way in to the AI revolution already and I don’t see job losses today. I think it’s a myth. The people who claim job losses, assume normally that jobs don’t change at all and they will be automated. But they miss the new jobs being created. I am confident that as the evolution unfolds and decides to become much more efficient to AI, we are going to find much more new jobs but education is key. In the past, we assumed a single education is enough for your life. That is going to go away.

          Where do you think India stands in terms of AI-usage and innovation compared to major economies like the US or China?

          I would think India is a country in transition. A country that comes from a situation that was hampered by difficulties, that was not moving to the forefront. I can tell you, the Infosys campus which I visited today, I was blown away. I think there is a culture emerging here of economic opportunity, there is a culture emerging of having your own massive companies that come straight from India, like Flipkart, Ola. They compete very well with global companies. If you can keep the momentum as a country, you are going to be there with China and the US very quickly. That’s my personal assessment, I am not an economist (smiles).

          How important is the government’s role in the adoption of AI?
          Recommended By Colombia


          I would argue that private industry and academia is better positioned than the government. People have called for regulations. I think it’s too early. I think the time to put regulations is when technology is abused by people. AI, like any technology, can be abused or even my kitchen knife can be abused in a negative way.


          AI is a tool that allows computers to form their own rules. It’s very good in specific domains for example in journalism, there is an AI system to help journalists write faster but that system can’t drive a car or fly a plane. What’s not there at all is self-consciousness, the ability to take over human life. All of this is from science fiction. In the field of general AI that solves all the problems like a human brain, the progress has been very bad. So, when people fear AI is going to enslave us, I think don’t worry about it right now. Ask me in 10 years again, maybe there’s some progress.


          How’s your flying experiment going with Kitty Hawk Corporation?


          Having been late for this interview due to traffic, it’s become clear to me this has to come to Bengaluru. Traffic is getting worse and worse. We have now prototypes that can fly at 200-300 km per hour, electrically—nearly inaudible at a range of more than 100 kms and then land and take off vertically. The technology is there but it’s not at scale yet, it’s not safe, it’s not quite affordable yet. All these things will happen and then the question is which government wants to stick out its neck. From a need perspective, Bengaluru, Delhi or Mumbai should raise their hands and say absolutely we should do it (laughs).

          New e-comm policy to help regulate domestic players

          Sidhartha
          Sources said that in the coming days, the government is also going to stipulate statutory audit under the Foreign Exchange Management Act to ensure that compliance of the rules is audited.
          | TNN | Feb 25, 2019, 06.32 AM IST
          NEW DELHI: The government will regulate the business of domestic e-commerce players only after a new policy is in place as it will help create a level-playing field with companies such as Flipkart and Amazon that have overseas investors.

          Amid concerns that domestic players do not have to comply with regulations such as mandatory sourcing from a larger pool of buyers, the government has expressed its inability to put in place regulations, at least immediately. "We first need to put in place regulations for the sector to provide a legal backing," an officer told TOI. Currently, only FDI rules have been notified.


          On Saturday, the department for promotion of industry and internal trade released a draft e-commerce policy for public comment, which is seen to be the basis for regulation of all players, both domestic and foreign. Amazon and Flipkart have been protesting the government's recent regulations, which mandated sourcing from more players as the two entities were seen to be running an inventory-based trading model instead of operating a marketplace.


          Sources said that in the coming days, the government is also going to stipulate statutory audit under the Foreign Exchange Management Act to ensure that compliance of the rules is audited. "This will help plug misuse," said an officer.


          The draft e-commerce policy explicitly said that FDI is only permitted in marketplaces. "An e-commerce platform, in which foreign investment has been made, therefore, cannot exercise ownership or control over the inventory sold on its platform," it said, while adding that these platforms should not adopt practices that favour only a select set of sellers. "FDI policy mandates fair and non-discriminatory treatment of all the stakeholders, including MSMEs and startups, operating on a marketplace... It is important to ensure that the letter as well as the spirit of the policy is met," the draft said.


          With the model code of conduct due to kick in early next month, the e-commerce policy is only expected to be in place after a new government takes office after the general elections.

          Harrods won't stop copycats in India: MD

          John Sarkar
          Several small outlets across India from apparel shops to sewing machine sellers use the 'Harrods' brand name but Ward, who was in the capital on Saturday to speak at The Global Business Summit, said it would be a waste of time and resources to register all the store's trademarks in the country.
          | TNN | Feb 25, 2019, 06.30 AM IST
          Harrods MD Michael Ward (File photo)
          NEW DELHI: Harrods MD Michael Ward said the iconic British luxury department store would not stop small businesses from mimicking the 'Harrods' brand name.

          Several small outlets across India from apparel shops to sewing machine sellers use the 'Harrods' brand name but Ward, who was in the capital on Saturday to speak at The Global Business Summit, said it would be a waste of time and resources to register all the store's trademarks in the country.

          "We would not stop people from doing that. Imitation is the sincerest form of flattery," he said. Other foreign brands, however, do not take imitation lightly. Seattle- headquartered Starbucks sued an Indian coffee chain SardarBuksh in July because the brand's name rhymed with its own.

          Although Ward said Harrods would want to be an early participant in India's growth story, he refused to divulge when the domestic luxury market could have its own Harrods on the lines of the one opened in China.


          "We have no plans for India. At the moment, we don't relate as well as we could do with HNIs in India. We started 10 years ago in China, where currently we represent 25% of the retail spend in the UK," he said.


          Harrods, owned by sovereign wealth fund Qatar Holding, is not too bullish on e-commerce either. "Our objective is to build long-term relationships with consumers, such as millennials, rather than selling a product immediately. People are coming to you for information and experience, and product may come from it, but it's not necessarily the first call," Ward said.


          "Our sales over telephone are bigger than our dot-com business, which is tiny. One of our biggest contracts this year was to design a house in West Asia," he added.
